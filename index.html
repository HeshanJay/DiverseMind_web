Our Research

Literature Survey

Over recent years, significant research has aimed to create innovative tools to address the unique needs of students. Most existing studies focus on diagnosed learning disabilities like dysgraphia and dyscalculia, leaving a gap in addressing slow learners without extreme conditions particularly in Sri Lanka. Available research either emphasizes identification or intervention, but rarely both.

“Writaupair” [15] is a web platform using Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques like dependency parsing and Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers (BERT) to assess writing difficulties and offer personalized support. Similarly, “IDeRBlog” [16] aids children aged 8-12 by enhancing spelling and writing via an intelligent dictionary that offers real-time feedback. However, these are language-specific and designed for extended writing like essays. In Sri Lanka, research has explored handwriting recognition and automated assessment tools for primary students. Study [17] developed a system using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) and Optical Character Recognition (OCR) to correct Sinhala handwritten responses and offer real-time feedback for Grade 5 learners. Similarly, study [18] introduced a Sinhala writing assistant for children aged 5-7, using an HTML5 canvas to analyze letter shape, pen movement, and line placement, providing visual feedback with an overall accuracy of 94.28%. However, both systems target general primary students and do not specifically support slow learners.

Another key challenge for slow learners is short attention span. According to [19], inattention is a symptom among preschoolers and not a disorder. Technologies now assess attention span using behavioral factors. For example, [20] measures it via eye and mouth ratios and head tracking, providing feedback to students and teachers. However, it only monitors physical cues, not emotional states, and targets general students. Similarly, [21] uses eye gaze and head pose to predict attention but doesn’t offer real-time feedback or focus on students. A more advanced system [22] evaluates attention in e-learning by analyzing blink rate, facial expressions, drowsiness, body movement, and noise detection, achieving 84.62% accuracy. Another system [23] combines eye gaze, blink rate, and facial expressions using OpenCV, CNN, and MediaPipe with a slightly better accuracy of 85%. Yet, neither [22] nor [23] utilize these insights to provide targeted interventions.

In primary-level mathematics, gamification has proven effective for slow learners. Tools like “NUMERATica” and “Tiki-Tik Matematika” use game-based learning with progressively challenging activities to enhance engagement and improve numeracy skills [24], [25]. Machine Learning (ML) also plays a vital role in screening learners. Support Vector Machines (SVMs) have shown high accuracy in predicting learning disabilities like dyscalculia [26], [27]. Cultural and linguistic context is crucial; “Nana Shilpa” successfully uses ML and localized Sinhala content to identify and assist Sri Lankan learners [27].

Working memory difficulties also pose a significant challenge for slow learners. Many struggle with problem comprehension and take longer to solve tasks [28]. AI-powered systems like PLAS [29] identify at-risk students through attendance, participation, and quiz scores, offering personalized support. However, it relies on historical data and lacks real-time assessment. At Mandalika University, psychological testing [30] revealed that 21.5% of students underperform due to low motivation and smartphone dependency. The “eLSIDA” system [31] used interviews and a gamified Learning Management Systems (LMS) for support, though it lacked data-driven cognitive assessments.

Thus, “DiverseMind” endeavors focusing on bridging the critical gaps by providing screening and intervention by addressing a range of unmet needs and challenges associated with supporting slow learners based on the following criteria

  • Writing Skills
  • Mathematical Skills
  • Attention Span
  • Short-Term Memory

Research Gap

Slow learners face significant educational challenges that require targeted, integrated solutions. This combines findings across four core components, writing, attention span, mathematics, and working memory to identify existing gaps in current research and tools, particularly in the Sri Lankan context. It highlights the need for a unified, contextually appropriate system to identify and support slow learners among primary school children.

WritingWriting is an expressive language skill that begins with toddlers’ scribbles [45], helping children communicate ideas and concepts [46]. One of the key traits of slow learners is difficulty with writing [11]. In Sri Lanka, many such students remain unidentified, even in leading schools. Early identification is critical to unlocking their potential, preventing future academic struggles, and boosting their confidence. Most existing research focuses on diagnosed learning disorders like dysgraphia [47], [48], [49], neglecting slow learners who face writing challenges without a formal diagnosis. While some international studies address this gap [50], [14], [51], Sri Lanka lacks localized research in this area. This leaves a significant gap in understanding and supporting this group. Additionally, current research is split some studies [14] identify writing issues, while others [11], [52], [53] propose solutions like the “doodling technique.” However, few combine both identification and intervention, creating a gap in integrated approaches. Most of these studies also target older students [54], [55], and rely on outdated methodologies [46], [11], rather than incorporating modern tools such as Machine Learning (ML).

Attention SpanAttention span is critical for effective learning, yet it is often overlooked in research concerning slow learners. Existing studies [56], [57], [58] focus on general students or those with ADHD, using tools such as eye-tracking. Consequently, the unique needs of slow learners remain unmet. While research like [59] explores the use of games to enhance attention in students with disabilities, these solutions are not adapted for slow learners or integrated with real-time monitoring. Although tools in studies [60], [34] employ advanced technologies like head pose detection or Kinect systems, they lack interactive features such as gamification, which could be beneficial in helping slow learners maintain focus. Moreover, no existing system combines detection with tailored support. This gap is particularly significant in Sri Lanka, where traditional classroom settings have limited resources and do not adequately support slow learners.

Mathematical ChallengesSlow learners often face mathematical challenges, yet there are notable gaps in both identification and intervention. Traditional methods such as teacher observations and performance records [61], [62] are slow and less accurate compared to technology-based solutions. In Sri Lanka, very few tech-driven tools are tailored to the local curriculum and Sinhala language, limiting their effectiveness. While studies [63], [64] praise the value of gamified tools, these tools are not adapted to the Sri Lankan context, reducing their practical usability. Furthermore, research typically separates identification [61] from intervention [21], [65], missing the opportunity to develop unified systems. Additionally, tools and resources referenced in [66], [67], [68] lack Sinhala language integration, making them less relevant for local application.

Working MemoryWorking memory is vital for learning, yet there is a lack of targeted research on slow learners in this area. Studies like [69], [70] use ML techniques to identify slow learners but fail to incorporate advanced models or dedicated working memory tests, reducing their accuracy. Tools like “eLSIDA” [71] provide interactive content but lack modern features such as real-time feedback or multisensory learning approaches. Some research, such as [72], use Fuzzy Logic to assess working memory, but does not integrate it with adaptive tools, limiting its practical application. While cognitive apps [73], [74], [75], [76] aim to improve general cognitive skills, they are not specifically designed for slow learners and often lack engaging elements. In Sri Lanka, where educational resources are limited, this gap becomes more pronounced.

These four components, writing, attention, mathematics, and working memory share critical gaps in current research and practice. Few systems integrate both identification and intervention. There is limited focus on younger slow learners, especially within the Sri Lankan education system. Studies frequently rely on outdated techniques, lack local relevance, and do not use modern technologies like ML, real-time feedback, and gamification. Global solutions often fail to align with Sri Lanka’s curriculum, language, and resource constraints.

To address these challenges, a web-based platform is developed specifically for Grade 4 students in Sri Lanka. This platform uses advanced technologies like ML algorithms, image processing, and multi-model architecture to identify and support difficulties in writing, attention, mathematics, and working memory. The system will provide solutions in Sinhala, aligned with the local syllabus, and include interactive features and real-time feedback. Developed with input from teachers and doctors, it aims to fill the critical gaps in current educational tools and improve learning outcomes for slow learners in resource-limited settings.

Research Problem

The term “learning difficulties” refers to a wide range of obstacles that children may face in their educational journey. These challenges may appear as difficulties in writing, reading, spelling, mathematics, memory recall, attention span, and learning speed, or may arise from auditory, visual, muscular, emotional, or neurological conditions. In Sri Lanka, nearly 30% of school-aged children are reported to have some form of learning difficulty [77], indicating the critical need for increased awareness and systematic, targeted interventions. Despite this high prevalence, many children with learning difficulties, particularly slow learners, remain unidentified and unsupported within traditional educational frameworks.

Slow learners, as defined by the International Literary Association, are students with Intelligence Quotient (IQ) scores ranging from 70 to 85 [77]. These students do not qualify for special education services but still face significant challenges in understanding academic content at the same pace as their peers. They require additional time, specialized support, and appropriate teaching strategies to thrive academically. Unfortunately, most conventional classroom settings in Sri Lanka follow a uniform teaching approach that fails to address the diverse learning needs of slow learners, leaving them vulnerable to academic failure and social isolation.

Although Sri Lanka has achieved a high net primary education enrollment rate of 99.11% in 2018, the transition to secondary education shows a notable decline, with enrollment dropping to 91.04% [78]. According to the United Nations Children’s Fund (UNICEF), children with learning difficulties, including slow learners, are particularly at risk during this phase due to inadequate inclusivity practices. The lack of proper identification and intervention mechanisms contributes to increased dropout rates and limits the long-term academic and social potential of these children.

Moreover, there is currently no integrated system in place within Sri Lanka to accurately detect and support specifically slow learners at an early stage. Most existing approaches are fragmented, outdated, or limited in scope, failing to incorporate modern tools and methodologies such as machine learning, or gamified interventions. This gap is particularly prominent in Sri Lankan primary schools, where fourth-grade students (typically aged nine) often struggle without timely support. Educators in these environments lack both the tools and the training to identify learning difficulties early, leading to missed opportunities for intervention.

Therefore, there is an urgent need to develop a comprehensive, contextually relevant system designed to detect and support slow learners in Sri Lankan primary education. Such a system should integrate modern technologies and align with the local curriculum to provide inclusive, effective, and sustainable educational solutions for the students. Given these gaps, the research problem addressed in this study is “How to provide a web-based application as a solution to effectively identify slow learners by evaluating their writing skills, attention span, mathematical abilities, and short-term memory, and provide interventions to improve their skills?” This research aims to fill the current void by proposing a digital platform tailored to the Sri Lankan educational context, supporting early identification and intervention through an engaging and data-driven approach.

Main Objective

The main objective of this research is to develop a web-based platform to identify slow learners among primary school children in Sri Lanka who face difficulties in traditional classroom environments. The application aims to assess and address the key learning challenges faced by slow learners namely attention span, writing abilities, mathematical skills, and short-term memory. By utilizing advanced Machine Learning (ML) algorithms, image processing techniques, and a multi-modal system architecture, the platform will conduct accurate assessments and provide targeted interventions. These interventions are designed to be age-appropriate, engaging, and effective. By incorporating gamification-based methods, the system seeks to transform the learning experience into an interactive and enjoyable process, thereby increasing student motivation and academic engagement. The ultimate goal is to create a supportive and inclusive educational environment that not only identifies learning difficulties early but also enhances the academic performance and confidence of slow learners through personalized support.

From the main objective, four specific objectives have been derived to provide a complete and practical solution through this research. These objectives focus on identifying and addressing the core learning difficulties faced by slow learners in primary education.

Objective: Writing Skills

Identify slow learners by assessing writing skills and provide engaging activities to enhance their writing skills.

Description

Slow learners with writing difficulties are children who face challenges in developing the skills necessary for effective written communication. These difficulties can occur in various forms, such as poor letter formation, difficulty in writing within given lines, and struggling with forming words, applying punctuation. Despite their challenges, these children are often incorrectly categorized as weak students due to their slower pace in acquiring writing skills compared to their peers. Unfortunately, most of the times teachers may unintentionally neglect these students, focusing on more in those learn quickly, which can further negatively affect the academic progress of slow learners.

Early identification of children with writing difficulties is critical for maximizing their learning potential. By recognizing and addressing these challenges at an early stage, teachers and parents can implement targeted intervention strategies that not only prevent future academic struggles, but also boost the child’s confidence. Therefore, the main objective of this component is to identify children with writing challenges among grade 4 primary school students and provide engaging activities designed to enhance their writing skills. The system, developed in the Sinhala language, aims to detect slow learners with writing difficulties early on, offering tailored interventions that support their development. By focusing on these students, the system ensures they receive the necessary guidance to improve their, eventually maximizing their learning potential and boosting their confidence.

Objective: Attention Span

Identify slow learners through attention span evaluation and provide engaging activities to enhance their attention level.

Description

In Sri Lanka, many students face short attention span challenges which one of the significant reasons for slow learning and some even face conditions like ADHD. These students deserve the same educational opportunities as others, as targeted interventions can increase their learning skills. Therefore, the most important objective here is to facilitate these students by early and timely identification and interventions. Therefore, through a web application this research aims to evaluate the attention span of slow learners using the latest technologies in the domain of face detection, gaze estimation and head posture. Based on this evaluation, it will provide gamified activities in Sinhala to improve attention and cognitive skills. By making the learning process more engaging and adaptive, we hope to improve educational outcomes among slow learners and make their daily lives in school easier. This approach not only supports their academic growth but also fosters their ability to adapt and succeed in life.

Objective: Mathematical Abilities

Identify slow learners by assessing mathematical abilities and provide engaging activities to enhance their mathematics skills.

Description

In Sri Lanka, many primary school learners face difficulties in mastering mathematics, which significantly hinders their academic development and self-esteem. The primary purpose of this study is to build a web-based tool named “DiverseMind” that adeptly detects and aids fourth-grade pupils who exhibit a slower learning pace. This tool will leverage sophisticated machine learning methods to evaluate students’ mathematical proficiency, pinpointing those who lag behind, and deliver customized educational exercises to strengthen their math capabilities.

The tool is intended to bridge a notable deficiency in the existing educational framework by merging detection and support features within a single system. It will be developed in Sinhala, the local language, and tailored to match the Sri Lankan school syllabus and educators’ guidelines, ensuring its relevance to the cultural and educational context. By offering stimulating and interactive math tasks, the application seeks to elevate academic performance, enhance self-worth, and foster a nurturing setting for students encountering these obstacles. Ultimately, this endeavor aspires to advance equitable access to education and nurture mathematical competence among slower learners, thereby supporting their overall scholastic progress and seamless participation in everyday classroom routines.

Objective: Working Memory

Identify slow learners through working memory assessment and provide engaging activities to enhance their working memory.

Description

Identify slow learners through comprehensive working memory assessments and enhance their working memory capabilities. The major aim of this project is to identify the slow learners age of 9 and deliver the productive and efficient solution as interventions through the web application. By leveraging machine learning techniques and cognitive assessments, the goal is to accurately pinpoint students who may require additional educational support due to limitations in their working memory. This objective also includes the development and implementation of tailored interventions, such as gamified activities and multisensory learning strategies, designed to specifically address and improve the working memory of these identified slow learners. Ultimately, the research aims to create a supportive educational environment that not only identifies cognitive challenges but also actively works to mitigate them, thereby enhancing the overall learning outcomes for slow learners.
